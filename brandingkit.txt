Meta Africa Sports – Brand Identity & Guidelines
Brand Identity System
Color Palette
MAS Red – HEX #CF2627, RGB (207, 38, 39), CMYK (0%, 82%, 81%, 19%). A bold, vibrant red conveying passion and energy.
MAS Orange – HEX #F26722, RGB (242, 103, 34), CMYK (0%, 57%, 86%, 5%). A bright orange for enthusiasm and action.
MAS Gold – HEX #F2C54E, RGB (242, 197, 78), CMYK (0%, 19%, 68%, 5%). A golden yellow evoking elite status, success, and optimism.
Accent Teal – HEX #2A9D8F, RGB (42, 157, 143), CMYK (73%, 0%, 9%, 38%). A complementary cool tone for digital highlights, balancing the warm palette and reinforcing a sense of trust.
Neutrals – White (HEX #FFFFFF) and Charcoal Black (approx HEX #1A1A1A) serve as primary background and text colors. Light Gray (e.g. HEX #F5F5F5) can be used for secondary backgrounds or UI elements to let primary colors stand out.
Color Usage Guidelines
Primary vs. Secondary Usage: The three brand colors (Red, Orange, Gold) form a cohesive gradient-inspired palette. Use MAS Orange as the primary accent for calls-to-action and interactive elements (e.g. “Sign Up” buttons or links) due to its high visibility. MAS Gold works well for backgrounds or data visualizations where a softer, prestigious highlight is needed. MAS Red is powerful – reserve it for emphasis in small doses (like important icons, alerts, or to mark premium content), so it draws attention without overwhelming.
Backgrounds & Contrast: On light backgrounds (white or light gray), the full-color MAS logo and palette maintain clarity. On dark backgrounds (charcoal or black), consider using the logo’s light/bright elements (e.g. the golden or orange tones) in isolation or adding a thin light outline so it remains legible. Maintain sufficient contrast: for example, use white text over MAS Red/Orange fills, and dark text over Gold or light backgrounds.
UI Application: Apply the Accent Teal sparingly to complement the warm palette – for instance, as a hover state for links, or an icon color in data charts – to convey a modern, trustworthy feel without diluting brand colors. Neutral tones should dominate areas with lots of text for readability (e.g. use white or light gray for page backgrounds, with black/charcoal text). The bright brand colors should then be used for data highlights, badges, or infographic elements, ensuring important numbers or labels pop.
Calls-to-Action: Default to MAS Orange for primary CTA buttons and banners – it’s vibrant and draws the eye. For hover or active states, you can darken the orange slightly or use the MAS Red as a hover effect to indicate interaction. Secondary buttons (less prominent actions) might use MAS Gold or a neutral gray outline, to distinguish from primary CTAs while still aligning with the palette.
Consistency: Whichever color is used in a given context, apply it consistently. For example, if MAS Gold is chosen for section headers or infographic icons, use it for all similar headers/icons to create a uniform look. Avoid using non-brand colors; the introduction of the accent teal is to be the only additional hue and should not compete with the core warm tones. This controlled palette usage will reinforce a cohesive and recognizable brand appearance across all materials.
Typography System
Headline Font
For headlines and titles, use a strong, bold sans-serif typeface that projects authority and modernity. Recommended: Oswald Bold (a condensed, uppercase-friendly font) for its impactful, athletic style. Oswald’s bold weight in all-caps delivers a commanding presence that is ideal for headlines, page titles, or player name callouts. It ensures headlines are instantly eye-catching and legible across web and print. If Oswald is unavailable, alternatives like Anton or Bebas Neue (also bold, condensed sans-serifs) can be considered for a similar authoritative look.
Body Font
For body copy and longer text, choose a readable, digital-optimized font that remains legible on screens of all sizes. Recommended: Open Sans Regular (or Roboto as an alternative) for paragraphs, descriptions, and data content. Open Sans is a clean, humanist sans-serif that renders well on both desktop and mobile, ensuring that scouting reports and player bios are comfortable to read. It pairs well with Oswald by providing contrast – a lighter weight and more neutral style for longer text – while still feeling modern and professional. Use the body font also for subheadings, captions, and any data tables, to prioritize clarity.
Font Hierarchy & Usage
Establish a clear typographic hierarchy using size, weight, and case to distinguish levels of information across web, mobile, and print:
H1 (Page Titles): Use the headline font (Oswald) at the largest size (e.g. ~48px on web, scaled to ~32–36pt for print). Consider using all caps or title case in Oswald Bold for a dramatic, authoritative title on landing pages or section headers. Maintain generous spacing around it (and letter-spacing if all-caps) to enhance its impact.
H2 (Section Headings): Also in the headline font, slightly smaller (e.g. ~32px web). H2s can be Oswald Semi-Bold if a slightly lighter weight improves readability, but still maintain that bold look. Use title case for a polished appearance. On mobile, scale H2 down slightly to perhaps 24px to fit smaller screens, but keep it distinguishable from body text.
H3/H4 (Subheaders): These can transition to the body font in Bold to improve legibility at smaller sizes. For example, use Open Sans Bold or Semibold for H3 (around 24px web) and H4 (18px). This creates a hierarchy where the most important headings use the display font, and secondary headings use the body font in a heavier weight. This way, hierarchy is conveyed both by size and by the font change.
H5/H6 and Minor Titles: Use the body font (Open Sans) Medium or Semibold, 16px or 14px as needed. These should be in sentence case for readability. For instance, a player card subtitle or a table heading could be Open Sans Semibold 14px. Ensure there is sufficient contrast (through color or weight) between these and the regular body text.
Body Text: Open Sans Regular 16px for web (approximately 10pt for print, adjusting for medium). Line-height should be around 1.5 for comfortable reading of scouting reports or news articles. On mobile, body text can be 14px–16px for readability without zoom. Maintain paragraph spacing to break up text blocks (short 3-5 sentence paragraphs for digestibility).
Captions and Footnotes: Open Sans Regular or Italic at ~12px (smallest size) for captions, image credits, or data source notes. Use a neutral gray color for less emphasis, but ensure it’s still legible (e.g. #666666 on a white background).
Additional Tips: Use consistent case and styling to reinforce hierarchy. For example, use all-caps Oswald for a SHORT TAGLINE or UI BUTTON LABEL, but use sentence case Open Sans for longer explanatory text. Avoid mixing too many fonts; stick to the two primary typefaces to keep the look clean and professional. Ensure sufficient contrast between text and background (especially with small fonts) – e.g., white text on MAS Red should be bold and maybe one or two points larger than usual to remain crisp. By following this hierarchy, whether it’s on a responsive website or a printed scouting pamphlet, readers will intuitively grasp the structure of information (what is a title vs. a subtitle vs. body copy) at a glance.
Brand Voice & Messaging
Tone & Style
Meta Africa Sports’ brand voice should be consistent, professional, and aspirational, instilling confidence in its audience. The tone can be defined by four key traits:
Credible & Authoritative: All messaging should sound trustworthy and expert. Use confident language, concrete facts, and a matter-of-fact tone that establishes MAS as the premier authority on African basketball talent. Avoid slang or hype; instead, statements should be backed by the verified data and scouting insight MAS provides. For example, highlight verified stats or expert opinions in the voice (“We meticulously verify each player’s performance data”), positioning MAS as a reliable source. This credible tone sets MAS apart from more casual, player-driven platforms – it speaks from the perspective of seasoned scouts and coaches.
Global & Inclusive: The voice should reflect a global reach and perspective. MAS is connecting Africa’s talent with the world, so the language should be inclusive and internationally appealing. Avoid region-specific jargon; instead, emphasize universal values like excellence, opportunity, and connection. The tone remains respectful of all cultures and highlights Meta Africa Sports’ role as a bridge between continents. (For instance, referring to “the global basketball community” or “international recruiters” in messaging.) This global tone conveys that MAS operates at an elite worldwide standard, welcoming NCAA coaches in the U.S., pro teams in Europe, and beyond.
Aspirational & Inspiring: While maintaining professionalism, the voice should inspire and motivate. There is an underlying passion and optimism in the wording – reflecting the dreams of young African athletes and the excitement of discovery. Use uplifting language that speaks to potential (“unlocking talent,” “rising stars,” “bright futures”). The tone should celebrate success stories and the journey from local courts to global stages. For example, MAS might say, “Empowering the next generation of African stars to reach their full potential.” This aspirational angle encourages players and reassures recruiters that MAS is about elevating talent to new heights.
Transparent & Engaging: Given the emphasis on trust, the voice is also transparent and open. Speak directly to the audience (using “you” where appropriate to engage NCAA coaches, scouts, or partners). Be clear about processes (“Every scouting report is verified by seasoned professionals”) and forthcoming with information. The style can be conversational but in a polished way – for instance, a welcoming note on the platform might read, “Join us as we shine a light on Africa’s top talent and connect you with players you need to know.” The aim is to sound inviting yet confident, positioning MAS as a trusted partner in scouting.
Overall, keep messages concise and impactful. The audience (coaches, recruiters, investors) are busy professionals – the voice should get to the point quickly, highlight what makes MAS valuable, and do so in a tone that blends expert authority with passion for the mission.

Visual Style Guidelines
Logo Usage
The Meta Africa Sports logo (both the full-wordmark and the circular “badge” emblem) should be used in a consistent and respectful manner to maintain brand integrity:
Color Variations: The primary logo features the signature color blend (MAS Red to Orange to Gold). This version works best on light backgrounds (white or light gray) to preserve contrast. When placing the logo on a dark background (e.g. black or dark photo), use an alternate version: for instance, a monochrome white logo, or a version where the warm colors are outlined in white for clarity. This prevents any parts of the logo from disappearing against dark colors. Never place the full-color logo on a clashing background (for example, avoid putting it on top of busy, multicolor imagery) – if it must sit on an image, consider adding a semi-transparent dark overlay behind the logo for legibility.
Clear Space: Maintain a safe zone around the logo equal to at least the height of the “M” in “Meta” (or the full diameter of the round badge) on all sides. No text, graphics, or edges of a canvas should encroach on this space. This ensures the logo isn’t crowded and stands out clearly. For example, when the logo is used on a flyer or webpage, ensure other elements are given a wide berth around it.
Sizing: The logo should always be large enough to be easily legible. As a rule of thumb, avoid using the full wordmark smaller than ~2 inches (5 cm) in print or ~150px width on screen – beyond that, the tagline or smaller text in the logo (if any) may become unreadable. The circular badge (icon) can be used at smaller sizes (down to about 40px on digital) as an avatar or watermark, since it has less small detail. Test legibility at different sizes; for very small uses (favicon, app icon), prefer the simple badge icon rather than the full text.
Dos and Don’ts: Always use the official, high-resolution logo files. Do not stretch, squash, recolor, or add effects to the logo. For instance, don’t apply drop shadows or glows that aren’t part of the original design. Do not alter the logo’s orientation or proportions – keep the aspect ratio locked. Also, avoid separating the logo elements (for example, don’t use parts of the wordmark in isolation unless that’s an approved alternate logo lockup). Consistency in presentation will build brand recognition.
Background control: When using the logo over images or videos (e.g., a highlight video thumbnail), pick an area of the image that is clean and provides contrast (like a darker corner if using the colored logo in full). Alternatively, use a solid brand-colored background behind the logo in those contexts. For example, many brands use a white version of the logo over their primary color as an intro or outro slide – MAS could use a white logo over MAS Red or Orange in a video ending slate. This ties the logo usage back into the brand palette.
By following these rules, the logo will always appear clear, professional, and instantly identifiable with Meta Africa Sports across all mediums.

Embrace contrast and saturation to a degree that the images feel vibrant and alive (showcasing the energy of the sport), but avoid filters that are too extreme or change the colors drastically (the jerseys and environments should look realistic).
